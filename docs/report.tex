\section{Introduction}

The time-independent Schrödinger equation in one dimension is a central equation in quantum mechanics. In dimensionless units ($\hbar = m = 1$), it can be written as

\begin{equation}
\psi''(x) = 2\,[V(x) - E]\,\psi(x),
\end{equation}

where $V(x)$ is the potential and $E$ is the energy eigenvalue.

Finding bound states corresponds to solving a boundary value problem where only specific values of $E$ produce physically acceptable (normalizable) solutions. This project implements a numerical approach based on the Numerov method combined with a shooting technique to compute these eigenvalues and eigenfunctions.

\section{Numerical Method}

\subsection{Numerov Algorithm}

The Numerov method is a finite-difference scheme designed for second-order differential equations of the form

\begin{equation}
\psi''(x) = q(x)\psi(x).
\end{equation}

It achieves higher accuracy by incorporating information from neighboring points. For a uniform grid with spacing $h$, the recurrence relation is

\begin{equation}
\psi_{n+1} = \frac{2(1 + \frac{5h^2}{12}q_n)\psi_n - (1 - \frac{h^2}{12}q_{n-1})\psi_{n-1}}{1 - \frac{h^2}{12}q_{n+1}}.
\end{equation}

This method is well-suited for the Schrödinger equation due to its stability and accuracy.

\subsection{Shooting Method}

To determine eigenvalues, a shooting method is used. For symmetric potentials:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Even states satisfy $\psi'(0) = 0$
    \item Odd states satisfy $\psi(0) = 0$
\end{itemize}

Starting from $x=0$, the wavefunction is propagated outward using the Numerov method. The correct energy eigenvalue is found by requiring the wavefunction to decay at large $x$.

A bisection method is used to locate energies where the boundary condition at the edge changes sign.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Infinite Square Well}

The infinite square well provides a benchmark since the exact energies are

\begin{equation}
E_n = \frac{n^2 \pi^2}{8a^2}.
\end{equation}

The numerical results show excellent agreement with the analytical solution, with relative errors decreasing as the grid is refined.

\subsection{Harmonic Oscillator}

For the harmonic oscillator,

\begin{equation}
V(x) = \frac{1}{2}\omega^2 x^2,
\end{equation}

the exact energies are

\begin{equation}
E_n = \omega\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right).
\end{equation}

The computed eigenvalues match the expected spectrum, and the wavefunctions exhibit the correct number of nodes and symmetry.

\subsection{Double Well Potential}

A quartic double-well potential was also studied:

\begin{equation}
V(x) = ax^4 - bx^2.
\end{equation}

The results show pairs of nearly degenerate states, corresponding to tunneling between the wells. This behavior has no simple analytical solution and demonstrates the power of the numerical approach.

\section{Convergence and Accuracy}

Two main sources of numerical error were analyzed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Grid spacing ($h$): smaller spacing improves accuracy
    \item Domain size ($x_{\max}$): insufficient domain truncates wavefunctions
\end{itemize}

The results show systematic convergence as $h \to 0$ and $x_{\max}$ increases, consistent with expectations for finite-difference methods.

\section{Conclusion}

This project demonstrates that the Numerov method, combined with a shooting technique, provides an efficient and accurate way to solve quantum bound-state problems.

The implementation successfully reproduces known analytical results and extends naturally to more complex potentials. The method is robust, easy to implement, and well-suited for computational physics applications.
